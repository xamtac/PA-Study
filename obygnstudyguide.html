1. Normal Pregnancy & Prenatal Care

1.1. Gestational Age & Dating

  • Naegele’s Rule:
    • EDD (Estimated Due Date) = (First day of LMP) + 7 days – 3 months + 1 year.
    • Assumes a 28-day cycle with ovulation on day 14.
  • Ultrasound Dating:
    • First-Trimester Crown-Rump Length (CRL) is the most accurate (± 5–7 days).
    • Second-Trimester Ultrasound accuracy: ± 7–14 days.
    • Third-Trimester Ultrasound accuracy: ± 14–21 days.
    • Importance: Accurate dating reduces iatrogenic inductions, prevents unnecessary interventions for “suspected post-dates,” and improves scheduling of prenatal tests.

1.2. Maternal Physiologic Changes

  • Cardiovascular:
    • ↑ Blood volume (by ~50%), ↑ Cardiac output (by ~30–50%), ↓ SVR (systemic vascular resistance).
    • Resultant physiologic anemia (lower hematocrit due to plasma volume expansion).
  • Respiratory:
    • ↑ Tidal volume, mild respiratory alkalosis, ↑ minute ventilation.
    • Diaphragm elevated by enlarging uterus → decreased functional residual capacity.
  • Renal:
    • ↑ GFR, ↓ BUN/Cr, mild glycosuria can be normal but must differentiate from GDM.
  • GI:
    • Slowed gastric emptying and bowel transit (↑ risk of constipation, reflux).
  • Coagulation:
    • Hypercoagulable state (↑ fibrinogen, factors VII, VIII, X), protective for hemorrhage but ↑ VTE risk.

1.3. Prenatal Visits & Initial Labs

  • Frequency:
    • Every 4 weeks until 28 weeks.
    • Every 2 weeks from 28–36 weeks.
    • Weekly from 36 weeks until delivery.
  • Initial Labs:
    • CBC (check for anemia, baseline platelet count).
    • Blood type and Rh (prevent isoimmunization with Rho(D) immune globulin if Rh negative).
    • Antibody screen (irregular antibodies).
    • Infection screening: HIV, Syphilis (RPR/VDRL), Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg).
    • Rubella & Varicella titers to confirm immunity.
    • Urine culture or urine dip to screen for asymptomatic bacteriuria.
  • Ongoing Visits:
    • Fundal height measurement (beginning ~20 weeks; height in cm ≈ gestational age).
    • Fetal heart rate (110–160 bpm is normal).
    • Maternal vital signs (especially BP).
    • Weight and urine dip (protein, glucose).

1.4. Screening Tests & Genetic Screening

  • Optional/Recommended Genetic Tests (varies by risk factors, patient preference):
    • Cystic Fibrosis (CF) carrier screening.
    • Hemoglobinopathies (e.g., sickle cell, thalassemias) based on ethnicity.
    • First-Trimester Screen (PAPP-A, β-hCG, nuchal translucency ultrasound).
    • Cell-free fetal DNA testing (cfDNA) for aneuploidies (trisomies 21, 18, 13).
    • Quad Screen in second trimester (AFP, β-hCG, Estriol, Inhibin A).
  • Timing:
    • 10–13 weeks: Nuchal translucency + PAPP-A + hCG (or cell-free DNA).
    • 15–20 weeks: Quad screen ± further imaging.

1.5. Nutritional & Lifestyle Guidance

  • Folic Acid: 0.4–0.8 mg/day to prevent neural tube defects; 4 mg/day if prior child with NTD.
  • Iron: Supplement if anemic; prenatal vitamins typically contain ~27–30 mg iron.
  • Calcium: 1000–1300 mg/day to support fetal bone growth.
  • Vitamin D: ~600–800 IU/day; consider higher in deficiency.
  • Exercise: Moderate intensity, avoid contact/high-impact sports; continue pre-pregnancy routines if safe.
  • Immunizations:
    • Influenza (inactivated) annually.
    • Tdap between 27–36 weeks each pregnancy.
    • COVID-19 vaccines as recommended by CDC/ACOG.
  • Avoid Teratogens:
    • No alcohol or illicit drugs; minimize caffeine (<200 mg/day).
    • No smoking (↑ risks of IUGR, abruption, preterm birth).
    • Medications: Avoid ACE inhibitors, isotretinoin, warfarin, etc.

1.6. Fetal Well-Being & Surveillance

  • Fetal Movement Counts (“kick counts”).
  • Non-Stress Test (NST):
    • Reactive (reassuring) = ≥2 accelerations of FHR (15 bpm above baseline for 15 seconds) in 20 min.
    • Non-reactive → further testing (BPP or CST).
  • Biophysical Profile (BPP):
    • NST + ultrasound parameters (fetal breathing movements, gross body movements, fetal tone, amniotic fluid index).
    • Score out of 10; ≤4 is worrisome.
  • Doppler Velocimetry for high-risk pregnancies (e.g., IUGR, preeclampsia).
  • Contraction Stress Test (CST): Observes FHR changes with uterine contractions.

2. Complications of Pregnancy

2.1. Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM)

  • Screening:
    • At 24–28 weeks with 1-hour 50 g glucose challenge test (GCT).
    • If ≥140 mg/dL (some use 130 or 135 mg/dL cutoff), proceed to 3-hour OGTT.
    • 3-hour OGTT (100 g) diagnostic criteria (Carpenter & Coustan):
      • Fasting ≥ 95 mg/dL
      • 1-hour ≥ 180 mg/dL
      • 2-hour ≥ 155 mg/dL
      • 3-hour ≥ 140 mg/dL
      • (2 or more abnormal values = GDM)
  • Management:
    • Dietary modifications, glucose monitoring (fasting + postprandial).
    • Insulin is first-line pharmacotherapy if glycemic targets not met with diet.
    • Oral agents (e.g., metformin, glyburide) used, though insulin often preferred.
  • Risks:
    • Fetal macrosomia, neonatal hypoglycemia, birth trauma (shoulder dystocia).
    • Long-term risk of T2DM in mother and child.

2.2. Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy

  • Chronic Hypertension: BP ≥140/90 before pregnancy or <20 weeks gestation.
  • Gestational Hypertension: New onset ≥140/90 after 20 weeks without proteinuria or other systemic findings.
  • Preeclampsia:
    • BP ≥140/90 after 20 weeks + proteinuria (≥300 mg/24 hr) or end-organ dysfunction (thrombocytopenia, renal insufficiency, impaired liver function, pulmonary edema, cerebral/visual disturbances).
    • With Severe Features: BP ≥160/110, severe headaches, RUQ/epigastric pain, elevated liver enzymes, etc.
  • Eclampsia: Preeclampsia + seizures (generalized tonic-clonic).
  • HELLP Syndrome: Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets.
  • Management:
    • Mild preeclampsia near term: Delivery at 37 weeks.
    • Severe preeclampsia: Control BP (IV labetalol, hydralazine, or PO nifedipine), Magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis, expedite delivery (≥34 weeks).
    • Eclampsia: IV magnesium sulfate + antihypertensives, immediate delivery once stable.
    • Long-Term: Women with preeclampsia have ↑ risk of cardiovascular disease later in life.

2.3. Preterm Labor & Premature Rupture of Membranes (PROM/PPROM)

  • Definitions:
    • Preterm Labor: Regular uterine contractions with cervical change before 37 weeks.
    • PROM: Rupture of membranes before labor onset at term (≥37 weeks).
    • PPROM: Rupture of membranes before labor onset and before 37 weeks.
  • Diagnosis: Nitrazine test (pH), fern test, speculum exam for pooling.
  • Management:
    • Tocolytics (nifedipine, indomethacin, terbutaline, magnesium sulfate) to delay labor if no contraindications.
    • Corticosteroids (betamethasone or dexamethasone) if <34 weeks to enhance fetal lung maturity.
    • Magnesium sulfate for fetal neuroprotection <32 weeks (decreases risk of cerebral palsy).
    • Group B Streptococcus (GBS) prophylaxis if indicated.

2.4. Placental Abnormalities

  • Placenta Previa:
    • Placental tissue covering or near the internal cervical os.
    • Painless bright red vaginal bleeding in the 3rd trimester.
    • Diagnosis by ultrasound; avoid digital cervical exam if suspected.
    • Often requires cesarean delivery if complete or marginal covering.
  • Placental Abruption:
    • Premature separation of placenta from uterine wall.
    • Painful vaginal bleeding, uterine tenderness, hypertonic uterus, possible fetal distress.
    • Risk factors: Hypertension, trauma, cocaine use, smoking, history of abruption.
  • Placenta Accreta Spectrum:
    • Accreta (superficial attachment), Increta (invasion into myometrium), Percreta (through serosa).
    • Risk with prior C-section + placenta previa.
    • Can cause massive postpartum hemorrhage; may require hysterectomy.

2.5. Hyperemesis Gravidarum

  • Severe, persistent nausea/vomiting, weight loss >5% of pre-pregnancy weight, dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, ketonuria.
  • Differential: Rule out molar pregnancy, thyroid disorders, GI etiologies.
  • Management:
    • IV fluids, thiamine supplementation (to prevent Wernicke’s encephalopathy).
    • Antiemetics: Pyridoxine (vit B6) ± doxylamine, ondansetron, promethazine, metoclopramide.
    • Correct electrolyte imbalances (hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis).

2.6. Multiple Gestation

  • Types:
    • Dizygotic (fraternal): Two separate ova, two placentas, two sacs.
    • Monozygotic (identical): One fertilized ovum splits; various chorionic/amniotic configurations depending on timing of split.
  • Risks:
    • Preterm labor, low birth weight, gestational hypertension, preeclampsia, GDM.
    • Monochorionic twins risk Twin-Twin Transfusion Syndrome (TTTS).
  • Management:
    • More frequent ultrasounds to monitor fetal growth.
    • Surveillance for TTTS in monochorionic gestations.

2.7. Intrahepatic Cholestasis of Pregnancy

  • Generalized pruritus (often worse at night, on palms/soles), elevated bile acids.
  • Risks: Fetal distress, preterm delivery, meconium passage, stillbirth.
  • Management:
    • Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) to reduce bile acids and itching.
    • Close fetal monitoring, consider delivery at 36–37 weeks if severe.

2.8. Other Notable Obstetric Complications

  • Rh Isoimmunization:
    • Occurs if Rh– mother is exposed to Rh+ fetal blood → maternal anti-D IgG → hemolysis in subsequent pregnancies.
    • Rho(D) immune globulin (RhoGAM) at 28 weeks and within 72 hours postpartum if baby is Rh+.
  • ABO Incompatibility: Usually milder than Rh disease (mother is blood group O, baby is A/B).
  • Amniotic Fluid Abnormalities:
    • Polyhydramnios: >24 cm AFI; associated with GDM, fetal anomalies.
    • Oligohydramnios: <5 cm AFI; associated with rupture of membranes, placental insufficiency, fetal renal anomalies.

3. Labor & Delivery

3.1. Stages of Labor

  1. Stage 1: Onset of true labor → Full cervical dilation (10 cm).
    • Latent Phase: Slow cervical dilation up to ~6 cm; can last ~20 hr in nullips, ~14 hr in multips.
    • Active Phase: More rapid dilation from ~6 cm to 10 cm; ~1.2 cm/hr in nullips, 1.5 cm/hr in multips (traditional estimates).
  2. Stage 2: Full dilation → Delivery of fetus.
    • ~2 hr in nullips (3 hr with epidural), ~1 hr in multips (2 hr with epidural).
  3. Stage 3: Delivery of fetus → Delivery of placenta.
    • Usually <30 minutes. Check placenta for completeness.

3.2. Labor Analgesia & Pain Management

  • Epidural Analgesia:
    • Most common; can cause hypotension, possible prolongation of the second stage.
  • IV Opioids:
    • Used earlier in labor, watch for neonatal respiratory depression if near delivery.
  • Non-Pharmacologic:
    • Continuous labor support (doula), breathing techniques, hydrotherapy, massage.

3.3. Normal vs. Abnormal Labor Progression

  • Friedman’s Curve vs. Modern Curves:
    • More time allowed before diagnosing arrest disorders.
  • Arrest of Dilation: No cervical change despite adequate contractions for ≥2 hours in active phase.
  • Arrest of Descent: No descent of presenting part for ≥1 hour (nullips) or ≥30 min (multips) in second stage.
  • Three P’s: Power (contractions), Passenger (fetus size/presentation), Passage (pelvic anatomy).

3.4. Operative Delivery

  • Vacuum Extraction / Forceps:
    • Indications: Prolonged second stage, suspicion of fetal compromise, need to shorten second stage for maternal benefit.
    • Risks: Maternal lacerations, fetal scalp/cephalohematoma, intracranial hemorrhage (rare).
  • Cesarean Delivery:
    • Indications: Failure to progress, non-reassuring fetal status, malpresentation, placenta previa, etc.
    • Complications: Infection, hemorrhage, anesthesia risks, future placenta accreta.

3.5. Fetal Monitoring & Interpretation

  • External vs. Internal: External uses Doppler for FHR, tocodynamometer for contractions; internal uses fetal scalp electrode + IUPC.
  • Baseline Fetal Heart Rate: 110–160 bpm.
  • Variability: Absent, minimal (<5 bpm), moderate (6–25 bpm), marked (>25 bpm). Moderate is reassuring.
  • Accelerations: Generally reassuring.
  • Decelerations:
    • Early: Mirror contractions; head compression → benign.
    • Variable: Abrupt drops; cord compression.
    • Late: Occur after peak of contraction; uteroplacental insufficiency → worrisome.
  • Category I, II, III:
    • Category I: Normal, reassuring.
    • Category II: Indeterminate, requires interventions/monitoring.
    • Category III: Abnormal, indicates fetal distress → immediate action, possibly emergent delivery.

4. Postpartum Care & Complications

4.1. Immediate Postpartum Period

  • Uterine Involution: Uterus descends ~1 cm/day; by ~2 weeks postpartum, no longer palpable abdominally.
  • Lochia: Vaginal discharge postpartum.
    • Lochia rubra (red/bloody, ~3–4 days).
    • Lochia serosa (pink/brown, up to ~10 days).
    • Lochia alba (yellow/white, weeks).

4.2. Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)

  • Definition:
    • ≥500 mL after vaginal delivery,
    • ≥1000 mL after cesarean, or
    • Any blood loss causing hemodynamic instability.
  • Causes (Four T’s):
    • Tone: Uterine atony (most common).
    • Tissue: Retained placental tissue.
    • Trauma: Lacerations, uterine rupture, hematomas.
    • Thrombin: Coagulopathies (DIC, von Willebrand disease).
  • Management:
    • Uterine massage, uterotonics (oxytocin, methylergonovine, carboprost, misoprostol).
    • Identify and repair lacerations; remove retained tissue.
    • Correct coagulopathy if present.

4.3. Postpartum Infections

  • Endometritis:
    • Infection of the decidua (uterine lining), more common after cesarean.
    • Presentation: Fever, uterine tenderness, foul-smelling lochia.
    • Management: Broad-spectrum antibiotics (clindamycin + gentamicin).
  • Mastitis:
    • Typically due to Staphylococcus aureus in lactating women.
    • Symptoms: Unilateral breast tenderness, erythema, warmth, fever.
    • Management: Antibiotics (dicloxacillin, cephalexin), encourage continued breastfeeding or pumping.
  • Wound Infections: At episiotomy or cesarean incision site → localized erythema, tenderness, possible drainage.

4.4. Postpartum Mood Disorders

  • Baby Blues: Mild depressive symptoms, resolves by ~2 weeks.
  • Postpartum Depression: More severe, lasting >2 weeks, may include anhedonia, guilt, possible thoughts of harm.
  • Postpartum Psychosis: Rare, severe; hallucinations, delusions. Medical emergency.

4.5. Lactation & Breastfeeding

  • Stages:
    • Colostrum first few days: High in antibodies.
    • Mature milk by ~2 weeks postpartum.
  • Benefits: Decreased infant infections, maternal weight loss, reduced risk of certain cancers.
  • Guidance: Breastfeed on demand (~8–12 times/day initially), ensure proper latch technique.

5. Contraception & Family Planning

5.1. Hormonal Contraceptives

5.1.1. Combined Oral Contraceptive Pills (COCs)

  • Estrogen + Progestin; suppress FSH/LH → prevent ovulation.
  • Benefits: Regulate menses, decrease dysmenorrhea, reduce ovarian/endometrial cancer risk, improve acne.
  • Risks: Thromboembolism, slight ↑ risk of breast cancer (controversial).
  • Contraindications: Smoker ≥35 (≥15 cigarettes/day), history of thrombotic events, migraine with aura, breast cancer.

5.1.2. Progestin-Only Pills (POP, “Mini-Pill”)

  • Indicated for breastfeeding women or those with contraindications to estrogen.
  • Thickens cervical mucus, partial ovulation suppression.
  • Must be taken at the same time daily; short missed-dose window.

5.1.3. Depot Medroxyprogesterone (Depo-Provera)

  • Injection every 3 months.
  • Side effects: Weight gain, irregular bleeding, possible bone density loss, delayed return to fertility.

5.1.4. Implant (Nexplanon)

  • Single-rod progestin implant in the upper arm, effective up to 3 years.
  • Side effects: Irregular bleeding, possible headache, breast tenderness.

5.2. Intrauterine Devices (IUDs)

5.2.1. Copper IUD (ParaGard)

  • Local inflammatory reaction toxic to sperm/ova.
  • Duration: Up to 10+ years.
  • Side Effects: Heavier, longer periods, dysmenorrhea.

5.2.2. Levonorgestrel IUD (Mirena, Skyla, Kyleena, Liletta)

  • Local progestin → thickens cervical mucus, thins endometrium, partial ovulation suppression.
  • Duration: 3–5+ years depending on brand.
  • Benefits: Lighter periods, possible amenorrhea, good for dysmenorrhea.

5.2.3. Contraindications to IUDs

  • Active pelvic infection (PID),
  • Uterine anomalies/fibroids distorting the cavity,
  • Unexplained uterine bleeding (investigate first).

5.3. Barrier Methods & Sterilization

  • Condoms (male/female): Only method also offering some STI protection.
  • Diaphragm: Fitted by provider, used with spermicide, must remain 6+ hours post-coitus.
  • Sterilization:
    • Female: Tubal ligation or salpingectomy.
    • Male: Vasectomy (verify azoospermia before relying on it).

5.4. Emergency Contraception (EC)

  • Levonorgestrel (Plan B) within 72 hours (OTC in many regions).
  • Ulipristal acetate (Ella) up to 120 hours (prescription).
  • Copper IUD (most effective) within 5 days; also provides ongoing contraception.

6. Gynecology: General Topics

6.1. Menstrual Disorders

6.1.1. Abnormal Uterine Bleeding (AUB)

  • PALM-COEIN classification: Polyp, Adenomyosis, Leiomyoma, Malignancy, Coagulopathy, Ovulatory dysfunction, Endometrial, Iatrogenic, Not yet classified.
  • Workup:
    • Pregnancy test, pelvic ultrasound, labs (CBC, TSH, prolactin), endometrial sampling if ≥45 or risk factors.
  • Management:
    • Hormonal therapy (COCs, progestin-only, high-dose estrogen for acute bleeding).
    • Tranexamic acid or NSAIDs for heavy menses.
    • Surgical options: Endometrial ablation, myomectomy, hysterectomy if definitive.

6.1.2. Dysmenorrhea

  • Primary: Painful menses without pelvic pathology (prostaglandin-mediated).
  • Secondary: Due to underlying pathology (endometriosis, fibroids, adenomyosis).
  • Management: NSAIDs, hormonal contraceptives, address underlying cause.

6.1.3. Amenorrhea

  • Primary: No menarche by 15 (with secondary sex characteristics) or 13 (without).
  • Secondary: No menses for 3 months (regular cycles) or 6 months (irregular cycles).
  • Workup: β-hCG to rule out pregnancy, TSH, prolactin, FSH/LH, check anatomic causes.

6.2. Menopause & Perimenopause

  • Definition: 12 consecutive months of amenorrhea, average age ~51.
  • Symptoms: Vasomotor (hot flashes, night sweats), mood changes, vaginal atrophy, osteoporosis risk.
  • HRT: Estrogen ± progesterone if uterus intact; watch for thromboembolism, breast cancer risk.
  • Alternatives: SSRIs (paroxetine), gabapentin, clonidine for hot flashes, topical vaginal estrogens for atrophy.

6.3. Pelvic Organ Prolapse

  • Types:
    • Cystocele (bladder → anterior vagina).
    • Rectocele (rectum → posterior vagina).
    • Uterine prolapse (uterus descends into vaginal canal).
  • Risk Factors: Multiparity, advanced age, obesity, chronic cough/constipation.
  • Management:
    • Pelvic floor exercises (Kegels), pessaries, surgical repair if severe.

6.4. Urinary Incontinence

  • Stress: Leakage with coughing, sneezing (urethral hypermobility).
  • Urge: Overactive bladder, detrusor overactivity → sudden urge to void.
  • Overflow: Chronic retention, incomplete emptying.
  • Management:
    • Stress: Kegels, pessaries, mid-urethral sling.
    • Urge: Bladder training, anticholinergics (oxybutynin), β3 agonists (mirabegron).

6.5. Pelvic Pain & Dysmenorrhea

Evaluate for endometriosis, adenomyosis, PID, GI or urinary causes. Primary dysmenorrhea often treated with NSAIDs, hormonal contraceptives.

6.6. Endometriosis

  • Ectopic endometrial tissue (ovaries, peritoneum).
  • Symptoms: Dysmenorrhea, dyspareunia, chronic pelvic pain, infertility.
  • Diagnosis: Laparoscopy is gold standard.
  • Management: NSAIDs, hormonal suppression, surgical ablation/excision if severe.

6.7. Uterine Leiomyomas (Fibroids)

  • Benign smooth muscle tumors of the uterus, estrogen-responsive.
  • Symptoms: Heavy menstrual bleeding, pelvic pressure, infertility (depending on location).
  • Diagnosis: Pelvic exam (irregularly enlarged uterus), ultrasound.
  • Management: GnRH agonists, surgical (myomectomy if fertility desired, hysterectomy if definitive).

6.8. Adenomyosis

  • Endometrial glands/stroma within myometrium.
  • Symptoms: Dysmenorrhea, menorrhagia, uniformly “boggy” enlarged uterus.
  • Diagnosis: MRI/TVUS can assist, definitive via histology (post-hysterectomy).
  • Management: NSAIDs, hormonal therapy, definitive = hysterectomy.

7. Gynecologic Oncology

7.1. Cervical Cancer

  • Etiology: HPV infection (types 16, 18, 31, 33).
  • Screening: Pap smear at 21; co-testing (Pap + HPV) at 30–65 if desired.
  • Abnormal results: ASC-US → reflex HPV or repeat Pap; LSIL/HSIL → colposcopy/biopsy.
  • HPV vaccine recommended at ages 11–12, up to 45 in some cases.
  • Management: Pre-invasive (LEEP, conization); invasive (hysterectomy or chemo/radiation depending on stage).

7.2. Endometrial (Uterine) Cancer

  • Risk Factors: Obesity, unopposed estrogen, PCOS, Lynch syndrome, nulliparity.
  • Presentation: Postmenopausal bleeding.
  • Diagnosis: Endometrial biopsy is gold standard, TVUS for endometrial stripe measurement.
  • Management: TAH-BSO ± lymph node sampling, possible radiation/chemo depending on stage.

7.3. Ovarian Cancer

  • Risk Factors: Family history, BRCA1/2, Lynch, increased ovulatory cycles.
  • Symptoms: Bloating, abdominal pain, early satiety, urinary frequency.
  • Diagnosis: Pelvic ultrasound, CA-125 marker, surgical exploration.
  • Management: Debulking surgery + platinum-based chemo, monitor CA-125 for recurrence.

7.4. Vulvar & Vaginal Cancers

  • Vulvar Cancer: HPV-related or lichen sclerosus; pruritus, ulcer/mass.
  • Vaginal Cancer: Rare, often metastatic from cervix or endometrium; bleeding, mass.
  • Diagnosis by biopsy, treatment with surgery ± radiation.

7.5. Gestational Trophoblastic Disease (GTD)

  • Hydatidiform Mole (complete or partial), Invasive Mole, Choriocarcinoma.
  • Complete Mole: 46XX (all paternal), no fetus, very high β-hCG, “snowstorm” ultrasound.
  • Partial Mole: Triploid (69XXY), some fetal tissue, lower malignant potential.
  • Management:
    • D&C, then weekly β-hCG checks until undetectable.
    • If β-hCG rises or plateaus → suspect persistent GTN → methotrexate chemo.

8. Breast Disorders

8.1. Benign Breast Conditions

  • Fibrocystic Changes:
    • Cyclical breast pain, lumps vary with menstrual cycle.
    • Ultrasound/mammogram for evaluation, biopsy if suspicious.
  • Fibroadenoma:
    • Solid, well-defined, mobile mass, common in younger women.
    • Usually painless. Confirm with imaging ± core biopsy.

8.2. Malignant Breast Disease

  • Risk Factors: Age, BRCA1/2, early menarche, late menopause, nulliparity, HRT, alcohol.
  • Screening: Mammograms starting at 40 or 50 (guideline-dependent), MRI in high-risk cases.
  • Common Types: Invasive ductal carcinoma (most common), invasive lobular carcinoma.
  • Management:
    • Surgery (lumpectomy + radiation or mastectomy).
    • Adjuvant chemo/hormonal therapy (e.g., tamoxifen, aromatase inhibitors).
    • Targeted therapy (HER2+ → trastuzumab).

8.3. Mastitis & Breast Abscess

  • Mastitis:
    • Infection (S. aureus) in lactating women; keep breastfeeding, antibiotics (dicloxacillin, cephalexin).
  • Breast Abscess: If fluctuant mass, may require ultrasound-guided aspiration or I&D.

9. Reproductive Endocrinology & Infertility

9.1. Definitions & Workup

  • Infertility: No conception after 12 months of unprotected intercourse if <35 yrs, or 6 months if ≥35 yrs.
  • Initial Workup:
    • Ovulatory function: mid-luteal progesterone, LH surge kits.
    • Semen analysis.
    • Hysterosalpingography (HSG) to check tubal patency.
    • Labs: TSH, prolactin, FSH/LH, estradiol, AMH.

9.2. Common Causes

  • PCOS (insulin resistance, anovulation).
  • Hypothalamic Amenorrhea (low GnRH due to stress, low weight, excessive exercise).
  • Hyperprolactinemia (inhibits GnRH → anovulation).
  • Tubal Disease (PID, endometriosis).
  • Male Factors (low count/motility).

9.3. Treatments

  • Ovulation Induction: Clomiphene citrate, letrozole.
  • ART: Intrauterine insemination (IUI), IVF, ICSI for severe male factor.
  • Referral to fertility specialist for advanced interventions.

10. Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs) & Other Gynecologic Infections

10.1. Common STIs & Treatment

  • Chlamydia: Azithromycin 1 g PO once or Doxycycline 100 mg PO bid x 7 days.
  • Gonorrhea: Ceftriaxone 500 mg IM once (if <150 kg) + Doxycycline if chlamydia not ruled out.
  • Trichomoniasis: Metronidazole 2 g PO once.
  • Bacterial Vaginosis: Metronidazole 2 g PO once or 500 mg PO bid x 7 days.
  • Syphilis: Penicillin G benzathine IM; dose/stage-dependent.
  • Herpes (HSV): Acyclovir or valacyclovir (episodic or suppressive).
  • HPV: Genital warts (types 6, 11) → cryotherapy or surgical removal; vaccination for prevention.

10.2. Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

  • Infection of upper genital tract (endometritis, salpingitis, TOA).
  • Pathogens: N. gonorrhoeae, C. trachomatis.
  • Symptoms: Cervical motion tenderness (Chandelier sign), pelvic pain, fever.
  • Management: Ceftriaxone IM + doxycycline ± metronidazole outpatient; IV regimen if severe.

10.3. Screening in Pregnancy

  • All pregnant women: Syphilis, HIV, Hep B, retest high-risk in 3rd trimester.
  • Gonorrhea/Chlamydia: Test high-risk populations.
  • Herpes: Not universal, but important to manage lesions near delivery.

11. Ectopic Pregnancy

11.1. Risk Factors

  • Prior ectopic, PID, tubal surgery, endometriosis, IUD in situ, IVF.

11.2. Clinical Presentation & Diagnosis

  • Unilateral pelvic/abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, possible referred shoulder pain if rupture.
  • Physical exam: Adnexal tenderness/mass, hemodynamic instability if ruptured.
  • Labs: Serial β-hCG, abnormal rise (<53% in 48 hrs). Transvaginal ultrasound essential.

11.3. Management

  • Medical: Methotrexate if stable, unruptured, β-hCG <5000, no fetal heartbeat, reliable follow-up.
  • Surgical: Salpingostomy or salpingectomy if ruptured or contraindications to methotrexate.
  • Follow-up: β-hCG must decline to undetectable to confirm resolution.

Final Tips for PANCE Preparation

  1. Stay Updated on Guidelines: Always check latest recommendations from ACOG, USPSTF, CDC.
  2. Master Common Scenarios: GDM, preeclampsia, postpartum hemorrhage, abnormal uterine bleeding, STIs, cervical cancer screening.
  3. Pharmacologic Details: Mechanisms, contraindications, side effects of key drugs (e.g., contraceptives, antibiotics).
  4. Differentiate Normal vs. Abnormal: Normal pregnancy physiology and labor progress vs. pathological findings.
  5. Practice Questions: Use clinical vignettes to build confidence in diagnosis and management steps.
